Identification

Autographa californica - (Speyer, 1875); Alfalfa Looper [Common Silver Y].

Forewings are mottled with black, brown, and gray.  Specimens have a slight purple tint and may have a silvery sheen.  The silvery-white stigma is forked on top and terminates in a rounded tip as it extends away from the body.  It can be separated from the nearly identical A. pseudogamma by a horizontal black dash that occurs near the upper, outermost corner of the forewing.  This black dash connects the subterminal and postmedian lines. Wingspan is 4.0 - 4.2 cm. 

Larvae are green and have three pairs of white lines on the back and a fairly wide white lateral line.  The head is brownish-green and typically has black lines on the side.

 

 

Habitat

Found in all habitats from steppe and cropland to above timberline.  One of the most widespread and abundant moths in western North America.

Similar Species

Autographa pseudogamma, Rachiplusia ou.

Distribution

This species migrates annually to the Pacific Northwest.  Worn specimens are found in late March and early April in both Washington and British Columbia (see map below).  Rarely, a freshly emerged specimen is seen in early spring, indicating that pupa can over winter in mild winters. The species is found from Alaska east to Manitoba and south to southern California, Arizona, and New Mexico . There is a single record from southern Michigan , undoubtedly a stray migrant.

Life History

Crumb (1956) found the Alfalfa Looper multi-brooded in western Washington . Larvae newly hatched in mid April emerged as adults by late May. Thus the species can produce three generation a year in the Pacific Northwest. The larvae feed from mid April through October in the Pacific Northwest (Crumb, 1956) and are considered a pest of many forage and vegetable crops including alfalfa, cabbage, and peppermint. Eichlin & Cunningham (1978) give a comprehensive list of host plants from more than 10 genera of woody shrubs and 40 genera of herbaceous plants.
